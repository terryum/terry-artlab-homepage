## TL;DR

Tactile simulation is either too slow (FEM) or too simplified (collision-based) for RL. This work models ReSkin magnetic elastomer sensors using point-cloud penetration for normal forces and Coulomb-inspired shear forces, binarized for sim-to-real transfer, achieving 5000 FPS simulation and zero-shot in-hand translation on real hardware.

## Problem

Tactile sensing is essential for dexterous manipulation, yet a large gap persists between tactile simulation and reality. FEM-based approaches are accurate but too slow for RL training. Fast simulators like IsaacGym use collision geometry penetration which produces sparse, unrealistic shear force signals. Consequently, prior RL-based in-hand manipulation relied solely on normal forces or proprioception, and in-hand translation with shear force feedback had never been demonstrated.

## Key Idea

A novel sensor model approximates ReSkin tactile skin physics using point-cloud-based penetration. Three core design principles:

- **Compliance modeling**: Each taxel's sensing range extends beyond its collision geometry, allowing sensor activation without physical collision
- **Point-cloud penetration**: Object surfaces are uniformly sampled as point clouds; normal force is computed from summed penetration distances within sensing range
- **Coulomb-inspired shear**: Shear signals multiply penetration sums by object linear and angular velocities, preserving directional information

Final outputs are binarized — shear $S_{x}, S_{y} \in \{-1, 0, 1\}$, normal $S_z \in \{0, 1\}$ — to minimize the sim-to-real gap.

### Sensor Model Details

ReSkin adhered to the palm uses 16 magnetometers for 3-axis force measurement. In simulation, each taxel is modeled as a cylinder (r=1.5cm) with sensing range $R$ extended beyond collision geometry.

Shear signal:
$$S_{x,t} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} P_i}{D}(v_{x,t} + \omega_{x,t})$$

Normal signal:
$$S_{z,t} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} P_i}{D}$$

where $P_i = R - l_i$ is penetration distance, $D$ is object point density (total points / volume), and $v$, $\omega$ are object linear and angular velocities.

### Two-Stage Training Pipeline

1. **Oracle policy**: Encodes simulator privileged information (object position, velocity, size, mass, COM, friction) into 8D vector $z_t$. Trained with PPO.
2. **Tactile policy**: Transformer observation encoder takes proprioception $q_t$ and tactile signals $[S_x, S_y, S_z]$ as input. Trained with $L_2$ loss to replicate oracle's $z_t$ and $a_t$; control policy is frozen.

Training speed: 5000 FPS on 2× NVIDIA RTX-4090 with tactile sensors (~70% of proprioception-only speed).

### Real-World Binarization

Real ReSkin signals have significant hysteresis, so simple thresholding is insufficient. A time-derivative threshold is used: if the difference between signal history and current signal buffers exceeds the threshold, 1 or -1 is returned. Since ReSkin outputs at 78Hz (4× faster than the 20Hz policy rate), the delay is negligible.

## Key Results

- **In-domain (cylinder, 0° tilt)**: S3-Axis achieves +38% distance (+2cm) and +94% velocity (+0.16cm/s) over Proprio-Only
- **OOD average (30 rollouts/policy)**: S3-Axis achieves 93% average success rate, +51% distance increase over Proprio-Only
- **U3-Axis**: +60% average velocity over Proprio-Only, particularly strong on screwdriver (+32% velocity) and water bottle (+153% velocity)
- **Hammer at 20° tilt (extreme condition)**: S3-Axis achieves +149% distance and +20% success over next best policy. U3-Axis completely fails, indicating signed shear direction information is critical

## Achievements and Limitations

**Key achievements**
- First RL-tractable sensor model (5000 FPS) simulating shear + normal tactile skin
- First sim-to-real demonstration of in-hand translation with shear force feedback
- Systematic validation of 3-axis tactile superiority for OOD adaptation across 190+ real-world rollouts

**Limitations**
- Authors note: No vision input for precise goal specification; frozen policy during deployment (no online adaptation)
- 🤖 AI analysis: Binary tactile signals incur significant information loss. Extending to continuous signals may improve performance, but would re-open the sim-to-real gap — a fundamental tradeoff that remains unresolved
