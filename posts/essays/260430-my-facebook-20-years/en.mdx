These days I'm turning everything I have into data. The goal is to build a replica of myself. Work-related data was the easy part — email, GitHub, Google Drive, PDFs, almost all of my work has long been digital. Every file is connected to Claude Code, and both retrieving past work and producing new work happen through it. (I'll write about this separately later.)

The harder part has been personal data. For an AI agent to replicate me more completely, it needs to learn the thoughts I've held, the experiences and emotions I've lived through, and the way I express myself in between — but personal life mostly happens in analog.

Fortunately(?) I anticipated this future and spent the last twenty years synchronizing myself with Facebook. Especially before founding ART Lab in 2019, I spoke my mind without much filter. Thanks to that, my AI has had something to learn from. The statistics describing those twenty years turned out to be interesting enough to share here.

## 1. How many posts are there?

I joined Facebook in January 2008. Over roughly eighteen years since, there are 5,363 posts. That averages out to about one post a day. During my PhD years in Canada, sitting at the computer twenty-four hours a day, the average climbed to two or three a day.

## 2. What kinds of posts dominated?

![](/posts/260430-my-facebook-20-years/img/00_pie_bar_combined.png)

In the beginning I was just casually sharing my life, so daily emotion (579) and daily events (470) make up a large slice. Back when I thought it was an intellectual's role to raise their voice on social issues, I wrote a lot about politics (536) and society (451). Closer to my actual profession, the categories pile up too: academia (518), tech/industry (489), research (335), and learning/meta-cognition (367). After founding ART Lab, most posts were startup-related — entrepreneurship/management (401) and organizational leadership (307) reflect just how much of my mind was on running a company.

## 3. When did I write the most?

![](/posts/260430-my-facebook-20-years/img/05_timeseries_category_stacked.png)

I joined in 2007, but Facebook only became fun for me from 2010. Like everyone else, I started by sharing daily life with friends (I probably shared a bit too much, ha). From around 2012 I started writing blog posts and columns, and going abroad in late 2014 made everything explode. During my PhD I was glued to the computer, so naturally I had a lot of distractions and stray thoughts, and I poured them into Facebook. 759 posts in 2016, 928 in 2017, 868 in 2018. During those years I wrote freely not just about life and tech but also social and political opinions.

I cut back sharply when I founded ART Lab in 2019. My voice was no longer just my own — it had become the voice of the company I represented. To avoid causing unnecessary worry to teammates and investors, I tried to limit anything frivolous. 294 posts in 2020, 181 in 2021, 110 in 2022, 65 in 2023, 60 in 2024, 35 in 2025... The higher up I went in a large company, the quieter I got. But in an AI-agent era where data is precious and attention is scarce, I think it's time to leave more traces again.

## 4. Did the character of the posts change with time?

![](/posts/260430-my-facebook-20-years/img/06_timeseries_category_lines.png)

Of course it did. Early on, Facebook was pure fun for me. Around 2010–2012 I left a lot of unselfconscious daily emotion (the blue line) — "this is fun," "I'm lonely," "anyone want to hang out?" Then as I started my PhD, talk of academia (green), the tech industry (red), and learning/meta-cognition (gray) climbed. The brown line (society) and orange line (politics) also rose — I believed back then that intellectuals had a duty to speak boldly on social matters.

After founding the company, almost nothing was written outside of the entrepreneurship/management line (pink). That was partly intentional, but partly because everything I cared about was the lessons of running a startup. Lately I've been trying to write about the tech industry (red) again, because I find the world that AI agents and Physical AI are reshaping genuinely interesting.

Looking at the graph, I miss the twenty-something who showed himself transparently without thinking too much. I miss the early-thirties who voiced socially critical convictions without hesitation. The mid-thirties startup founder full of worry was a meaningful chapter too. And now in my forties, an executive at a large company... life only gets heavier, but maybe I'm finally starting to understand what "an adult's responsibility" means.

Reviewing twenty years of my Facebook in data brings up a lot. Both the changes in volume and the changes in content replay in my head like a film of where I bent and where I straightened.

It's been a generous platform. Thanks to Facebook I connected with great people, found good opportunities, and received a lot of encouragement and support. I'm full of gratitude. I started as a young man whose only job was to enjoy himself, became a PhD student pouring out everything, then a founder wrestling with hard questions, and now I'm an office worker uncle.

People used to tell me, both in the ART Lab days and now, "Why bother writing on social media? It only invites trouble." But I'm going to write again. As the past twenty years of Facebook data became a mirror, I'd like the next twenty to be one too. And I look forward to the better connections and opportunities that find their way through.
