Reading and writing by hand have become a luxury in this era.

Ask AI one small question and it floods you with information. Since you can't rely on a single AI, you cross-check with GPT, Gemini, and Claude — tripling the text you need to read. The answers are blunt, never quite satisfying in one pass. A few more rounds and your reading stamina is spent. Coding is the same story. The labor used to lie in the precise weaving of code; now it's shifted to reading AI's plans, checking its progress, and reviewing its output. Add social media's "breaking news" and "one-page summaries," and some days end with nothing but reading.

But does reading this much actually make you smarter?

Back in college entrance prep, I once read a handbook that condensed 100 literary classics into one page each. I gained far less than someone who had read even a single book from cover to cover. The same goes for skimming 100 news feeds or watching 100 short-form videos. No matter how many layers of summary you stack, depth never appears. Even if you subscribe to every paper summary newsletter, it can't match reading one deep paper all the way through — because an author's depth can only be absorbed through the reader's own depth.

Writing has a similar trap. A few sentences tossed to AI and it drafts something for you, creating the illusion that it can free you from the slow agony of choosing each word. But in practice, the loop of receiving drafts, editing, sending back, receiving again — you end up reading and writing several times more. Just because something can be generated in an instant doesn't mean the process is proportionally more productive.

What does it mean to write? It is the process of pulling tangled thoughts out of your head, examining them, assembling scattered pieces, discarding the residue, and putting only the distilled essence back inside — a 'thought refining process.' Even if multi-agents burning through tokens like sparks within harness engineering can produce a book's worth of text in minutes, this single page I'm writing by my own hand carries an irreplaceable meaning that belongs only to me.

So I decided to read lightly and write lightly, every day. The density of knowledge is not proportional to the number of characters processed per hour. Even if slow, I will read by my own eyes and write by my own hand, steadfastly guarding my mind soaked in the deluge of noise.
