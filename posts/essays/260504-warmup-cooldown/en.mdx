If exercise is the metaphor, Claude Code is the main set — the part where calories burn hardest. The output of several agents burning tokens at once is overwhelming. Code piles up. Docs appear. Tests run.

But the main set alone doesn't build a body.

Anyone who has trained knows this. The main set burns the most calories, yes, but skip the warm-up and you lose days to injury; skip the cool-down and tomorrow's intensity collapses. Sustainable upward growth doesn't come from the intensity of the main session — it comes from the rituals at its two ends: warming the body while planning today's work, and afterward sorting through what was learned and what was missing.

Lately I keep dropping both ends. The morning where I open Obsidian and quietly write what to do today. The evening where I slowly walk back through what I did. I skip those two beats and dive straight into the main set. Open Claude Code, throw in a task, watch the tokens burn. Hands fast, head empty.

The problem is that burning many tokens doesn't mean the work is done. Come evening, the screen is full of artifacts, but it's hard to answer what I actually finished today. The session was intense, but I can't be sure the direction was right. Intensity and progress are different axes. I already named this trap in [Work That Isn't](/en/posts/260422-work-that-isnt), but naming it wasn't enough to climb out.

The warm-up is a ritual for setting direction. With a clean head, write today's coordinates: what's important, what's urgent, what must happen daily over the long arc. Only then does the intensity of the main set carry meaning. Intensity without coordinates is thrust without direction.

The cool-down is a ritual for cooling that intensity. Slowly look at where the tokens went. Separate progress from drift. Did I get carried away into side paths? Am I satisfied with output that strayed from the original goal? Only after asking does tomorrow start differently from today.

The faster the tool in your hands, the more you need both ends of the ritual. The stronger the tool, the longer the distance of directionless thrust — and the further you travel toward the wrong coordinates. You may flatter yourself that you burned plenty of tokens and worked hard, when in truth you may have spent the day absorbed in things that didn't need to be done. The stronger the main set, the heavier the rituals at its ends must be. Break this proportion and you won't see returns to match the strength of the tool.

Tomorrow morning, before lighting up the tokens, I'll open Obsidian first and quietly write down what to do. I shouldn't be in such a hurry...
