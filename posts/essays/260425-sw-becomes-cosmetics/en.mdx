The cost of building software is converging fast toward zero. Burn tokens and code comes out; review can be settled with tokens too. Nothing is left to slow the production line, and so software is being shipped cheaper, faster — a hundred-, thousand-, ten-thousand-fold per unit time. Building software is no longer scarce. What is not scarce, multiplies.

This curve is not new. Korea has already lived through it in the cosmetics market.

Korea has roughly thirty thousand registered cosmetics brand owners. More than the US, Japan, and the EU combined. Why are there so many cosmetics companies in Korea? Because in Korea, anyone can make cosmetics. Cosmetics ODMs make it so.

I work at Cosmax, the world's largest cosmetics ODM. There is Kolmar Korea at a similar scale, and around five thousand smaller manufacturers crowd into clusters like the Namdong Industrial Complex in Incheon. Making cosmetics requires no special skill: burn money and the ODM handles concept, R&D, first production, even logistics — one stop. With anyone able to enter, Korea ended up with thirty thousand brand owners.

The result was a fierce branding and marketing war. In a red ocean overflowing with product, two paths remain to survive: build a premium brand of your own, or transmit a sharp marketing message through Instagram, TikTok, home shopping — selling hard. The cosmetics market is the front line of branding and marketing. The cause of this war is that cosmetics stopped being a product only specialists could build, and became one anyone could.

For software too — easier to build, exploding in supply — the same two axes survive. Brand and marketing.

A brand is the compression of trust. Even when anyone can make it, people buy L'Oréal. Software will be the same. When everyone shaves the same features on top of the same models, users pick the familiar name and the familiar promise. Among infinite options, the first thing humans cut is the unverified name.

Marketing grows stronger. Just as an influencer can sell cosmetics without knowing the formula, software can be sold without knowing how it works. The ability to sell sits separately, and the ability to make is held up by an ODM. Canva pouring money into advertising lately is no accident — it is the signal that a market that competed on product is shifting into one that competes on marketing.

And the ODM is coming. Today's software industry is fragmented into scattered SI shops. A giant software ODM will emerge. People with only an idea and a sales channel will go to the ODM and order, "Build me one of these." It might be the evolved form of freelance platforms like Kmong. The form may differ, but the function is the same — turning the ability to build into outsourceable infrastructure.

This flow naturally extends what came [after coding was democratized](/en/posts/260415-democratization-of-research). When the ability to build becomes common, the axis of value moves to where the customer is — branding, marketing, sales.

Of course, not every kind of software will become cosmetics. Infrastructure software, software requiring deep domain depth, will trace different curves. But the vast majority of software ordinary people use gets pulled onto the Cosmax curve. So if you want to see the future of the software industry, look at the cosmetics industry. The two are following the same function with a lag.

That I have moved from robotics to AI, from AI to startup, and now into the middle of a cosmetics ODM — perhaps that, too, is not a coincidence.
