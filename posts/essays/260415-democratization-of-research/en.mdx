Coding was always closer to translation than creation. Taking what's in your head and converting it into an unnatural, logically flawless language that machines can understand. There was a wide gap between human thought and machine language, and the people who could bridge that gap were developers. That's why developers were valuable.

But AI, trained on massive amounts of code, filled that gap. If you have a blueprint that says "build this like that," it's a problem with a definite answer. Problems with answers get solved eventually, whether by humans or AI. Maybe it was never that hard to begin with — a problem with defined inputs and outputs, destined to be solved end-to-end.

Once AI made coding trivially easy, developers who had been treated as scarce commodities just a few years ago were suddenly no longer needed. To be more precise, only the 0.1% of elite programmers who can match AI's capability and the 2.9% who can leverage AI to do the work of a hundred are actually needed. The remaining 97% are practically unnecessary. If they're still employed, it's probably because someone didn't know better, or knew but couldn't find an alternative. In that case, you might as well just do it yourself.

In an era where anyone can build software, we are living in the age of democratized coding.

What comes next? I believe it's academic research.

Vast amounts of research results have already accumulated in the form of text — papers. Reading, understanding, and connecting all of them is precisely the kind of work AI can do far better than humans. As I wrote in [Brain Augmentation](/posts/260310-brain-augmentation), what matters in the AI era of research isn't "how much I personally know" but "how well I can build an environment where AI scientists can sustain self-improving knowledge creation." Given that environment, AI will conduct research far beyond my own intellectual capacity.

Of course, code and papers are different. Code gives you answers when you run it, but papers have subtext. Undisclosed experimental conditions, context only the authors know, logical leaps, and experiments that require physical execution. So AI can't yet replace 100% of human research. But I believe it will become possible in stages, and soon.

The first human research to disappear will be work that can be completed entirely within documents. Literature surveys, organizing and comparing existing research, connecting concepts across papers — AI already outperforms humans in these areas. (The [Surveys](/surveys) being written by AI on my homepage are proof.) Going forward, the job of reading documents and summarizing them for others will cease to exist. In an age where reading itself has become the bottleneck, reading and organizing for others is hopelessly inefficient. (They won't read it themselves anyway.)

The second stage is experiments conducted inside computers. AI model research, for instance, may seem impressive, but most of it is just changing model architectures, swapping evaluation metrics, and adjusting parameters through training. Humans kick off a training run, occasionally get up to check the results, make slight adjustments, and repeat. Does this really need to be done by a human?

Andrej Karpathy's [Auto research](/posts/2603-autoresearch-autonomous-ml) illustrates this shift well. Karpathy had AI repeat a cycle of "5-minute training → check results → modify code → 5-minute training," and in 2 days the AI ran about 700 experiments, reducing GPT-2 training time by 11%. Anthropic showed similar results — in [Automated Alignment Researchers](/posts/2604-automated-alignment-researchers), nine Opus 4.6 models conducted alignment research, discussed findings, and iterated on improvements, achieving a PGR of 0.97 in just 5 days compared to human researchers' PGR of 0.23. The message is clear: AI can handle computational experiments just fine without us.

The final stage is automating human experimental labor through physical AI. Until now, work outside the computer — running chemistry experiments, visually inspecting results, maintaining lab spaces, installing new equipment for new experiments — was something AI agents couldn't accomplish with a single click. Given how burdensome experimental labor is, the biology and chemistry domains have been advancing the concept of [Self-Driving Labs (SDL)](/posts/2604-self-driving-labs). Now we stand at the threshold of not just automated experiments, but the birth of true AI scientists with intellects surpassing our own.

What happens at the end of this progression is identical to what the democratization of coding showed us: just as average coders are no longer needed, average researchers won't be needed either. Just as junior developer hiring has plummeted, professors will stop recruiting graduate students. Going further — just as CEOs no longer need CTOs, corporations will no longer need research labs. The world will need only the 0.1% of researchers who can match AI's capability and the 2.9% who can leverage AI to do the work of a hundred. The remaining 97% of researchers will simply not be needed.

This may have been a foregone conclusion since the democratization of coding. In a world where showing an AI agent the destination of a software product means it quickly finds the How and builds the solution, wouldn't asking "remove this person's life bottleneck" — just like asking "remove this software's speed bottleneck" — make finding that How an AI's job? As AI models keep improving and humans connect with AI through [Neural Link](https://neuralink.com/) for decoding and debugging, perhaps even the bottlenecks of human life will eventually be resolved.

As code becomes cheap, so does knowledge. We stand on the cusp of the democratization of research. In a world where the value of knowledge rapidly converges to zero, intellectuals will need to keep moving — lest they be swallowed by the wave.
